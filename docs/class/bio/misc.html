Introduction

  1. Define biology.
  2. Explain how the study of biology could be used to improve and understand some aspect of the world.
  3. Name the organisms that were first on the planet.
  4. List the properties of life and find an example of each.
  5. List the levels of organization and find an example of each.
  6. Explain your relationship to all living things and how this information could be important.
  7. List some of your goals for this semester.
  8. Go to SI with Buhler!

Chapter 1.1

  1. Explain how the study of biology could be used to improve and understand some aspect of the world.
  2. Define science.
    1. Why is it important that science is systematic?
    2. Why is it important that science only addresses the natural world?
  3. Develop a criteria to tell if a question or study is scientific.
  4. Diagram the steps of the scientific process.
  5. Contrast the bias of humans in proving and disproving a claim.
  6. Compare and contrast a hypothesis with a theory.
  7. Design a controlled experiment with a hypothesis, null hypothesis, an independent variable and a dependent variable.
  8. Explain a null hypothesis.
  9. Explain some of the possible relationships between cause and correlation. 
  10. Explain the consequences of drawing conclusions and enacting policy using only corollary data.
  11. Develop criteria for recognizing pseudoscientific claims.
  12. Explain how scientists ensure that published research is scientific and credible.

2.1

  1. Draw the Bohr model of the atom.
  2. Create a chart for the 3 subatomic particles that includes:
    1. Mass
    2. Charge
    3. Location
  3. Calculate atomic mass.
  4. Define the atomic number.
  5. Define an isotope.
  6. Identify an element, its symbol, atomic mass and number using a periodic table.
  7. Discuss the importance of valence electrons in atomic interactions.
  8. Identify the number of valence electrons in an atom with the use of a periodic table.
  9. Define an ion and name the 2 types and 2 charges of ions.
  10. Explain the gain or loss of electrons in the 2 types of ions.
  11. Identify +1, +2, -1, and -2 ions on the periodic table.
  12. Generally speaking where are metals and non-metals located on the periodic table.
  13. Identify noble gases on the periodic table.
    1. Explain why noble gases don't readily bond with other atoms.

2.2

  1. Define a molecule.
  2. Define a covalent bond.
    1. Contrast non-polar and polar covalent bonds.
  3. Describe how ionic bonds form.
  4. Define what a salt is.
  5. Explain the adage "like dissolves like."
  6. Contrast the bond strength of non-polar covalent, polar covalent and ionic bonds in water.
  7. Describe the strength of a hydrogen bond and how they form.
  8. Describe the behavior of a very electronegative ion. List 3.
  9. Diagram the 3 parts of a chemical reaction.

2.3

  1. Draw a single molecule of water and label the atoms, bond types and partial charges.
  2. Draw two bonded molecules of water and label the atoms, bond types and partial charges.
  3. Contrast hydrophobic and hydrophilic substances and give examples of each.
  4. Describe 4 properties of water and how they are significant to living organisms and systems that support living organisms.
  5. Identify acids, bases and neutral substances by their pH.
  6. Name the ions that donated by bases and which ions are donated by acids.
  7. Identify acids, bases and neutral substances by their relative amounts of OH - and H + .
  8. How do acids taste?
  9. Define a buffer and explain why it is important to living systems.

2.4

  1. Identify 4 elements that make up >90% of biological matter.
  2. Identify carbon single, double and triple bonds in a molecule.
  3. Define and recognize isomers.

2.5

  1. Define an organic molecule.
  2. Explain carbon’s importance in forming biological molecules.
  3. Identify the 7 functional groups in a molecule. Identify which 1 is acidic and which 1 is basic.
  4. Name the 4 biological macromolecules.
  5. List the monomers and polymers of 3 of the biological macromolecules.
  6. Differentiate the biological macromolecules when presented with a set of molecules.

2.6

  1. Explain the significance of the Stanley Miller experiment.

3.1

  1. Define a gene.
  2. Identify the monomers and polymers of nucleic acids.
  3. Identify the parts of a nucleotide.
  4. Differentiate between DNA and RNA.
  5. Describe the importance of the 1’, 2’, 3’ and 5’ carbon of a nucleic acid.
  6. Name the 2 parts of a nucleotide that make up the backbone of a nucleic acid.
  7. Name the bonds that make up the backbone of a nucleic acid.
  8. Contrast a nucleoside with a nucleotide.
  9. Adding a nucleotide to a growing chain.
    1. How many phosphates must be on a nucleotide that is going to add to a growing nucleic acid chain?
    2. How many phosphates are removed from a nucleotide that adds to a growing nucleic acid chain?
  10. Identify the functional groups on the 5’ and 3’ end of a nucleic acid.
  11. Describe the characteristics of the DNA double-helix.
  12. Explain what antiparallel means.
  13. Identify purines and pyrimidines by their number of rings.
  14. Label the 5 different bases as purines or pyrimidines.
  15. Calculate the percentages of bases in a nucleic acid.
  16. Be able to base pair 2 complementary strands of DNA.
  17. Identify the number of hydrogen bonds between base pairs.
  18. Diagram the parts of the central dogma.

3.2

  1. Diagram explain the steps of the central dogma.
  2. Generally, contrast prokaryotic and eukaryotic cells.
  3. Generally, contrast the central dogma in prokaryotes and eukaryotes.

3.3 – 3.4

  1. Diagram transcription.
  2. Contrast the template and non-template (coding) strand.
  3. Identify a transcript, the template strand and non-template strand when given one of them.
  4. Identify the template, what’s synthesized the reading direction and synthesizing direction of RNA polymerase.
  5. Diagram the parts of a gene.
  6. Describe the 3 steps of transcription.
  7. Name the 4 groups of proteins that participate in initiation.
  8. Generally, contrast prokaryotic and eukaryotic cells.
  9. Generally, contrast transcription in prokaryotes and eukaryotes.
  10. Identify the location of the parts of the central dogma in prokaryotes and eukaryotes.
  11. Describe the 3 types of RNA processing in eukaryotes.
  12. Define alternative splicing.
  13. Explain the significance of alternative splicing and its relation to the diversity of eukaryotic proteins.

4.1

  1. Identify an amino acid among other molecules.
  2. Draw and label the parts of an amino acid.
  3. Memorize the number of standard amino acids.
  4. Memorize the names and charges of the basic and acidic amino acids.
  5. Explain how monomers and polymers are assembled into polymers and how polymers are broken into monomers and small polymers using water.
  6. Describe the 4 level of protein structure.
    1. Types of bonds
    2. Shapes
    3. Functionality
    4. Number of peptide chains
  7. Identify the functions of chaperones.
  8. Define denaturing and identify 3 properties that affect denaturing.
  9. Explain how changes in primary structure can lead to disease.

4.2

  1. Diagram the Central Dogma and circle the step of translation.
    1. Where does translation occur in a eukaryotic cell (in comparison to transcription)?
  2. Diagram the structure of the ribosome.
    1. Subunits
    2. Sites
    3. Which type of RNA makes up the ribosome?
  3. Explain why the ribosome is a major drug target
    1. What is the direct effect of damaging the ribosome?
  4. Diagram the structure of tRNA and describe its function.
    1. What end is the amino acid attached to?
    2. Circle where the anticodon located.
    3. Name the enzyme that attaches amino acids to tRNA.
      1. What is this process called?
    4. Contrast the general functions of mRNA, rRNA and tRNA.
    5. Contrast a codon with an anticodon.
      1. On which molecule is each located?
    6. Use the genetic code to determine amino acids, codons and anticodons.
    7. Memorize the start and stop codons.
      1. Memorize the name of the amino acid associated with the start codon.
      2. Memorize that stop codons do not code for an amino acid.
    8. Describe how the genetic code redundant.
      1. What is a benefit of this?
      2. What position shows the most flexibility in still coding for the same amino acid?
    9. Identify which strand the genetic code refers to specifically.
    10. Identify the 3 reading on a single strand.
    11. Calculate the number of amino acids, nucleotides or codons when given the number of one of them.
      1. Example: if a protein has 500 amino acids, how many codons code for this protein? How many nucleotides are there in the coding sequence of the mRNA?
    12. Describe the 3 steps of translation.
      1. Review where the eukaryotic ribosome binds from transcription lecture.
      2. In which ribosome site does the first tRNA enter?
      3. In which ribosome site does every tRNA enter after the first enter?
    13. Contrast the structure of monocistronic and polycistronic mRNA.
      1. Define an operon
      2. In which domains are monocistronic and polycistronic mRNA most prominent?
