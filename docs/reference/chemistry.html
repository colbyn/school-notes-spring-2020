Chemical Formulas

Molecular Formula

  • Shows actual number of atoms.
  • Used for covalently bonded atoms.

Empirical Formula

  • simplest whole-integer ratio of atoms in a compound.
  • Used for ionic compounds -crystals that can form thousands of bonds. Such as \(\mathrm{Na}_{1000}\mathrm{Cl}_{1000}\). The Empirical Formula simplified this into just \(\mathrm{Na}\mathrm{Cl}\).

Structural formula

  • Graphical diagrams.

Compounds

Molecular (Covalent)

$$\begin{equation} \begin{split} \text{nonmetal} + \text{non-metal} &\implies \text{molecular compound} \end{split} \end{equation}$$
  • Held together because they are sharing electrons.

Ionic

$$\begin{equation} \begin{split} \text{metal} + \text{non-metal} &\implies \text{ionic compound} \end{split} \end{equation}$$
  • Electrons get stolen, and opposite sides attract.
  • Made of lattice structures.
  • Compounds with polyatomic ions are classified as ionic.

Terminology

Solutions

  • Solutions have previously been defined as homogeneous mixtures.
  • The relative amount of a given solution component is known as its concentration.
  • The solvent is some \(C_x\) that is significantly greater than all other \(C_{xs}\) in the given solution.
  • A solute is a component of a solution that is typically present at a much lower concentration than the solvent.
    Solute concentrations are often described with qualitative terms such as dilute (of relatively low concentration) and concentrated (of relatively high concentration).
  • This component is called the solvent and may be viewed as the medium in which the other components are dispersed or dissolved.
  • A solution in which water is the solvent is called an aqueous solution.

Dilution of Solutions

  • Dilution is the process whereby the concentration of a solution is lessened by the addition of solvent.
  • Dilution is also a common means of preparing solutions of a desired concentration.
    By adding solvent to a measured portion of a more concentrated stock solution, a solution of lesser concentration may be prepared.
  • A simple mathematical relationship can be used to relate the volumes and concentrations of a solution before and after the dilution process.

According to the definition of molarity, the molar amount of solute in a solution (n) is equal to the product of the solution’s molarity (M) and its volume in liters (L):

$$\begin{equation} \begin{split} n &= ML \end{split} \end{equation}$$

the dilution equation is often written in the more general form:

$$\begin{equation} \begin{split} C_1 V_1 &= C_2 V_2 \end{split} \end{equation}$$

where \(C\) and \(V\) are concentration and volume, respectively.

Mass-Volume Percentage

  • A mass-volume percent is a ratio of a solute’s mass to the solution’s volume expressed as a percentage.

Parts per Million and Parts per Billion

  • Very low solute concentrations are often expressed using appropriately small units such as parts per million (ppm) or parts per billion (ppb).
  • The ppm and ppb units defined with respect to numbers of atoms and molecules.
  • Both ppm and ppb are convenient units for reporting the concentrations of pollutants and other trace contaminants in water.
$$\begin{equation} \begin{split} \mathrm{ppm} &\equiv \frac{\text{mass solute}}{\text{mass solution}} \times 10^6 ppm \\ \mathrm{ppb} &\equiv \frac{\text{mass solute}}{\text{mass solution}} \times 10^9 ppb \\ \end{split} \end{equation}$$

Units & Values

Atomic Mass Unit (amu)

  • Equivalent to Dalton (Da) and Unified Atomic Mass Unit (u).
$$\begin{equation} \begin{split} 1\;\mathrm{amu} &= \frac{1}{12}\;\text{mass of carbon-12}\\ &\approx 1.6605 \times 10^{-24}\mathrm{g} \end{split} \end{equation}$$

Formula Mass

$$\begin{equation} \begin{split} \small{\text{Let}}\;\;&\\ A &= \text{“atoms in a given substance”}\\ A_{\mathrm{avg}} &= \text{“avg. mass of each atom in $A$”}\\ \small{\text{Therefore}}\;\;&\\ \text{“Formula Mass”} &= \text{“sum of all values in $A_{\mathrm{avg}}$”} = \text{“Molecular Mass”} \end{split} \end{equation}$$

The Mole

  • An Actually Good Explanation of Moles (YouTube) -since we can't just count how many atoms we have for a given sample, we instead use e.g. grams to measure how many atoms we have.
    “Person 1: how much oxygen do we have there? Person 2: well you how the atomic mass of oxygen, so that, but in grams.” Moles are means of saying just such.
$$\begin{equation} \begin{split} 1\;\mathrm{mole} &\approx 6.022 \times 10^{23}\\ &\approx 6.02 \times 10^{23}\\ &\approx \small{\text{“Avogadro’s number”}} \end{split} \end{equation}$$

Note that

$$\begin{equation} \begin{split} 1\;\mathrm{mole} & \implies \text{“amount” unit}\\ \end{split} \end{equation}$$

Therefore given any two elements \(E_1\) and \(E_2\)

$$\begin{equation} \begin{split} \text{$1$ mole of $E_1$} &= \text{$1$ mole of $E_2$} \end{split} \end{equation}$$

Molar Mass

  • The molar mass of an element (or compound) is the mass in grams of \(1\) mole of that substance.
  • This property is expressed in units of grams per mole, \(\frac{\mathrm{g}}{\mathrm{mol}}\)
  • The mass of one mole of atoms is called the molar mass.
  • The molar mass of an element is grams is numerically equal to the element's atomic mass (amu). e.g.
    $$\begin{equation} \begin{split} 1\;\mathrm{mole}\;\text{of carbon} &= 12.011\;\mathrm{grams}\\1\;\mathrm{atom}\;\text{of carbon} &= 12.011\;\mathrm{amu} \end{split} \end{equation}$$
  • \(1\;\mathrm{mole}\;\text{of carbon} = 12.011\;\mathrm{grams}\); while the weight (in amu) of \(1\;\text{carbon} = 12.011\;\mathrm{amu}\). Technically, the mass of \(1\;\mathrm{mole}\) of a given substance should be denoted as \(\frac{\mathrm{g}}{\mathrm{amu}}\).

Notes

$$\begin{equation} \begin{split} \text{“molar mass of a compound $C_x$ in grams”} &= \text{“formula mass of $C_x$ in amu”}\\ \text{atomic mass} &= \text{molar mass} \end{split} \end{equation}$$

Percent Composition

  • Works for Molecular and Empirical Formulas.
  • Can be used for determining an Empirical Formula from Percent Composition.

Given some compound \(C_x\)

$$\begin{equation} \begin{split} \text{“Percent Composition”} &\equiv \text{“percentage by mass of each element in $C_x$”} \end{split} \end{equation}$$

Therefore given some \(E_x \in C_x\)

$$\begin{equation} \begin{split} \% E_x &\equiv \frac{\text{mass of $E_x$}}{\text{total mass of $C_x$}} \times 100\% \end{split} \end{equation}$$

Note that \(E_x\) may itself be a group of atoms in the case of Molecular and Empirical Formulas.

Derivation of Molecular Formulas

  • Molecular formulas are derived by comparing the compound’s molecular or molar mass to its empirical formula mass.
    As the name suggests, an empirical formula mass is the sum of the average atomic masses of all the atoms represented in an empirical formula.

Molarity (M)

  • Molarity is defined as the number of moles of solute in exactly 1 liter of the solution.
  • Uppercase M means “moles per liter”.
$$\begin{equation} \begin{split} \mathrm{M} &\equiv \frac{\text{mol solute}}{1\; L\;\mathrm{solution}} \end{split} \end{equation}$$

Tips

Given some element \(E\):

$$\begin{equation} \begin{split} E_m &= \text{amount of $E$ in moles}\\ E_g &= \text{amount of $E$ in grams}\\ E_{\text{amu}} &= \text{Atomic weight of $E$}\\ \text{Therefore}&\\ E_g &= E_m \times E_{\text{amu}}\\ \text{Or alternatively}&\\ \text{“Grams”} &= \text{“Moles”} \times \text{“Atomic weight”} \end{split} \end{equation}$$
